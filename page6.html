NBA history

The establishment of the league and the merger (1946–1956)
The BAA (Basketball Association of America), which later became the NBA, was founded in 1946, and Maurice Podoloff, who also served as president of the AHL ice hockey league, was appointed president. Unlike previous attempts to establish a national basketball league, the BAA league was the first to hold most of its games in large halls in large cities, such as Madison Square Garden and Boston Garden. The league consisted of 11 teams that played 60 games in the regular season, before competing in playoff series that determined the identity of the champion. On November 1, 1946, in the inaugural game of the BAA, the New York Knickerbockers defeated the Toronto Huskies 68–66 in front of 7,090 spectators at Maple Leaf Gardens in Toronto. Ozzie Schechtman of the Knickerbockers scored the first basket of the game, which is considered the first basket in NBA history. The first championship in the history of the league was won by the Philadelphia Warriors (now the "Golden State Warriors"), after beating the Chicago Stags 4-1 in the final series.
In its first years, the quality of the teams in the BAA league did not exceed that of teams in parallel basketball leagues, but the addition of several strong teams in the 1948/1949 season - Fort Wayne Pistons, Indianapolis Jets, Minneapolis Lakers and Rochester Royals -
Significantly raised the level of play and made the BAA a favorite destination for college players who wanted to turn pro.

In 1949, at the end of its third season, the BAA league decided to merge with the NBL league and establish the NBA league. The new league brought together six teams from the NBL, ten from the BAA and added the Indianapolis Olympians to create a league of 17 teams in total. As of 2015, there are six teams operating in the NBA league that took part in the BAA league. For the most part, the NBA considers the BAA as part of its own history.

In the 1950/1951 season, the number of league teams was reduced to 11, a trend that continued until the 1954/1955 season, at the end of which only 8 teams remained in the NBA. During this period, the Minneapolis Lakers, led by center George Macken, became the first dominant team in the NBA, winning five championships in six seasons (1949, 1950, 1952–1954).

In order to increase the pace of play in the NBA, in the 1954/1955 season, a shot clock was used for the first time, requiring the offensive team to shoot to the basket within 24 seconds. It was one change among several other changes in the basketball constitution that came into effect, such as the definition of the "color zone", where it is forbidden to stay for more than three seconds, which created a revolution in the NBA league. The average points per team in the period before the shot clock was about 79.5 points per game, and already in the 1954/1955 season the average of the teams rose to about 93.1 points per game.

The rise of the Boston Celtics and the expansion of the league (1957–1978)

In the 1956/1957 season, the strength of the Boston Celtics team began to rise, mainly due to the draft selection of center Bill Russell. Russell, along with the team's point guard Bob Causey and coach Red Auerbach, made the Celtics the dominant team in the league during the 1960s; By the 1969 season, Boston had won 11 championships, including eight in a row (1959-1966) that established the longest championship streak in NBA history. In the 1959 draft, the Philadelphia Warriors selected center Wilt Chamberlin, who became one of the dominant players in the league and set records for points per game (100) and rebounds per game (55) that have not been broken to this day. The rivalry that developed between Russell and Chamberlin, the two best players at the time who faced each other in eight playoff series, is considered one of the greatest rivalries in American sports history. With Russell's retirement in 1969, the Celtics lost their power and ceased to dominate the league. In the 1970s, for the first time since the league's first seasons, there was no particularly dominant team and eight different teams won the championship. During that time the league expanded significantly; Between the 1961 and 1971 seasons, 9 new teams joined, bringing the NBA to 17 teams in total.

In 1967, the ABA league was established, which was a parallel and competing league to the NBA. The competition between the leagues for viewers and broadcast contracts led to a fight to recruit the best basketball players. The great high school star of the era, Lou Alcindor (known as "Kareem Abdul-Jabbar"), did choose to join the NBA, but the ABA league attracted many top players, such as Julius Erving, George Garvin , Rick Barry and Moses Mellon. The struggle between the leagues to recruit players helped to increase competitiveness in the industry and eventually led to a considerable improvement in the terms of employment of the professional staff in the industry, including players and referees. In 1971, as part of the 25th anniversary of the founding of the NBA, the league introduced a new logo, which was inspired by the logo of the American baseball league. The likeness of Lakers star Jerry West was used as the model for the logo, which features a white silhouette of a basketball player on a red and blue background. In 1976, due to a lack of television broadcasting contracts and heavy financial losses, the ABA league ceased to exist as an independent body and merged into the NBA. Four of the ABA teams—the San Antonio Spurs, Denver Nuggets, Indiana Pacers, and New York Nets—joined the 22-team NBA after the merger. At the end of the 1970s, the popularity of the NBA was at a low point, which was manifested in low ratings and low occupancy of halls, among other things due to many drug scandals that put the league in a negative light.

The rise of modern basketball (1979–1999)

The 1979/1980 season is considered by many to be the beginning of modern basketball - characterized by a fast game that is fun to watch - following the entry into the league of two of the greatest players in NBA history - Magic Johnson and Larry Bird. Johnson and Bird were known for their great rivalry, which began when they met in the finals of the 1979 College Championship Tournament, and continued when the two led their teams to back-to-back NBA Finals appearances. During the 1980s, Johnson led the Lakers, along with Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, to five championships, while Bird won three championships with the Celtics. The joining of Bird and Johnson to the NBA greatly contributed to the growth of the league's popularity: after a decade in which the league suffered from a low level of interest and a drop in ratings, a new generation of fans grew following their arrival in the league and the rivalry that developed between them. Another factor that increased the popularity of the league during this period was the addition of the three-point arc that was used in the ABA league, with the aim of helping the shorter players to score more, space out the defensive game and encourage a more enjoyable game to watch.

In 1984, two more of the most prominent and influential figures in the league's history joined the NBA - incoming commissioner David Stern, and Chicago Bulls star Michael Jordan, who is considered the best basketball player of all time. Although Jordan failed in his attempt to lead the Bulls to the championship in his first years in the league, his athletic and virtuosic abilities increased interest in the NBA throughout America and even abroad. David Stern, as part of his role as the new CEO of the league, began a marketing and management revolution that included a number of successful initiatives, such as setting a salary cap for the league (in order to increase equality between the teams), applying strict drug tests to the league's players and intensive sales promotions in collaboration with commercial companies.

In 1988-1989, the Charlotte Hornets, Miami Heat, Orlando Magic and Minnesota Timberwolves were added to the league, expanding the number of NBA teams to 27 in total.

At the beginning of the 1991/1992 season, Magic Johnson unexpectedly revealed that he was carrying the HIV virus, the cause of AIDS, and announced his retirement from playing. Johnson's retirement, along with Bird's at the end of that season, left "control" of the league in the hands of Jordan and the Bulls, who won six championships in eight years (1991–1993, 1996–1998). Jordan's temporary retirement between the 1993 and 1995 seasons was used by the Houston Rockets team, led by center Hakeem Olajuwan, to win two championships (1994-1995). In 1996, as part of the NBA's 50th anniversary celebrations, a list of the 50 best players in the history of the league up to this year, called the "50 Legends", was selected.

The twenty-first century

After Jordan's second retirement and the dissolution of the Bulls team at the end of the 1998 season, the Los Angeles Lakers returned to being one of the leading teams in the league and won three consecutive championships (2000–2002). The most prominent players in its ranks were the center Shaquille O'Neal and the shooter Kobe Bryant, and its coach was Phil Jackson. After the departure of O'Neal and the addition to the team of the Spanish forward Pau Gasol, the Lakers won two more championships in the 2009-2010 seasons. Another prominent team during this period is the San Antonio Spurs, who won five championships (1999, 2003, 2005, 2007, 2014) under the guidance of coach Gregg Popovich and the leadership of forward Tim Duncan. In total, the Lakers and Spurs shared 10 championships between them between the 1999 and 2014 seasons, and apart from them, the only team that won more than one championship in this period is the Miami Heat, who won the title three times; In the 2005/2006 season, Dwyane Wade and Shaquille O'Neal led the Heat to the first championship in its history, and in the 2010/2011 season, Wade was joined by the pair of stars LeBron James and Chris Bosh, who helped the club win two more championships in the 2012-2013 seasons.

Other teams that won the championship in the 2000s are the balanced Detroit Pistons (2004), the Boston Celtics of the "Big Three" - Paul Pierce, Kevin Garnett and Ray Allen (2008) and the Dallas Mavericks led by the German forward Dirk Nowitzki (2011).

In the 2014/2015 season, the Golden State Warriors won the championship, led by the MVP of the league Stephen Curry, and in the following season continued to demonstrate dominance when they recorded the best regular season record in NBA history, 73-9. This historical balance did not help her win the championship that season, where she was defeated in the final series by the Cleveland Cavaliers led by LeBron James and Kyrie Irving. In the 2017 and 2018 seasons, Golden State, which won two more championships, and Cleveland, which faced them in the final series for the third and fourth time in a row, continued to stand out.

In the 2018/2019 season, the Toronto Raptors won the first championship in its history, after beating the Warriors in the finals series in their fifth consecutive appearance in the finals.

On March 11, 2020, the league announced the suspension of the rest of the games in the 2019/2020 season until further notice, following the infection of Utah Jazz player Rudy Gober with the corona virus. On July 30, the league resumed, when the 22 teams with a chance of qualifying for the NBA playoffs participated in eight more regular season games in the "bubble" that operated at Disneyworld in Orlando. On August 17, the playoffs began, which were also held in the bubble at Disney World, and at the end of the playoffs, the Los Angeles Lakers won the 17th championship in their history led by LeBron James and Anthony Davis.
